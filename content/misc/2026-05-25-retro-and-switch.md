## So What Are These Things?

They're called retro handhelds, and brands like Anbernic and Retroid have been quietly making them better and cheaper for years.

## Okay But What About Brand New Games?

Fair question. And yes — if you specifically want Nintendo's new releases, you need a Switch 2. That's a real reason to buy one.

But let's push on the assumption underneath that. Not "do you need new games" — that's the wrong question. The real question is: *is new actually more fun?*

PS2 and GameCube games weren't great because people were easier to please back then. They were great because the industry hadn't yet figured out how to ship half a game and sell you the other half later. No DLC. No season passes. No day-one patches fixing a broken launch. No live service draining your wallet on a monthly subscription. You bought a game, it was finished, and it had to be worth your time or word got out.

That discipline produced some of the most tightly designed, densely packed, endlessly replayable games ever made. Gran Turismo 4 has over 700 cars and real tracks. Burnout 3 has more content than most modern racing games at launch. F-Zero GX is harder and faster and more satisfying than anything in the genre released since. These games stand completely on their own feet — put one in front of someone who's never heard of them and they'll have a great time. That's the only test that matters.

There's also a law of diminishing returns at play here that the gaming industry would prefer you not think about too hard. The jump from the NES to the SNES was staggering. SNES to PS1 was mind-blowing. PS1 to PS2 felt like the future. But PS2 to PS3? Noticeably better. PS3 to PS4? Sure. PS4 to PS5? Most people couldn't tell from across the room. Each generation the leap gets smaller — but the price of hardware and games keeps going up. You are paying more and more for less and less of a difference, chasing a ceiling that's basically already been hit.

The modern gaming industry has trained us to equate "new" with "better." That's a marketing argument, not a design argument. And it's costing people $80 a title to find out the hard way.

## But What About Family Time?

Here's something that doesn't get talked about enough: both of these devices can turn into a full living room gaming setup in about 30 seconds.

Both the $45 Anbernic and the $249 Retroid have HDMI output, Bluetooth controller support, and WiFi. Plug either one into a TV, pair a couple of Bluetooth controllers, and you have a couch gaming setup. The Retroid can also take a USB-C hub with up to four wired controllers if you prefer that. Either way, the device becomes the console and the TV is the screen.

**Playing together in the same room** works beautifully on both. Games like Super Mario Kart (SNES), Bomberman, and Sunset Riders — a wild west shoot-'em-up where two players blast through waves of outlaws side by side — were designed from the ground up for exactly this kind of couch play. On the Retroid, you can step it up to Serious Sam: Next Encounter — two players on PS2, or four-player split-screen on the GameCube version, connected to the TV, mowing down hordes of insane aliens together. These aren't nostalgia picks; get a 10-year-old in front of any of them and watch what happens.

**Playing device-to-device on your home wifi** is also possible on both, through a feature called RetroArch Netplay. It's peer-to-peer on your own local network — no strangers on the internet, no servers, just two devices talking directly to each other in your house. Works well for 8-bit and 16-bit games. Competitive Mario Kart races, head-to-head Bomberman, chess via a native Android app like Chess.com. It's genuinely the same experience as sitting next to each other, just on separate screens — useful when two kids want their own device rather than sharing a TV.

One honest note: device-to-device netplay works best with the simpler older systems. For PS2 and GameCube multiplayer, you're better off connecting the Retroid to your TV and playing with Bluetooth or wired controllers from the couch — that's the more reliable and better-feeling experience for those generations anyway. That's a Retroid-only scenario given the $45 Anbernic can't run PS2.

The Switch 2 has polished local multiplayer too. But a second Switch 2 for another family member is another $450. Two Anbernic RG35XX H devices is $90 total. Two Retroid Pocket 6s is $500 combined — and that still includes free games forever. The math keeps working out the same way.

There are real reasons to buy one — just fewer than Nintendo's marketing would have you believe.

If you specifically want Nintendo's new exclusive releases as they come out, that's a legitimate reason. Nintendo still makes great games and nobody else has them. If you have older kids who are socially invested in whatever their friends are playing, that's real too — you're not just buying a game, you're buying them into a conversation.

If you want Nintendo's polished, officially licensed storefront — that's a real advantage. But it's worth asking honestly whether that curation is worth $450 upfront plus $80 a game, because that's exactly what you're paying for it. Retro handhelds ship preloaded with thousands of games, and adding more is as simple as dropping files onto an SD card. Free access to a massive library of genuinely great games versus a sleek modern store where the privilege of shopping costs a small fortune — for improvements most people won't even notice. People have different tastes, and that's fine. Just go in with eyes open.

Those are honest reasons. "It's new" and "it's Nintendo" on their own — probably not enough anymore.
