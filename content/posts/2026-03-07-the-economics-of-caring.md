There's a common complaint about Claude: rate limits, outages during peak usage, pricing tiers that remind you this isn't free. I've hit all of them. And yet I think these friction points are actually evidence of something good.

Anthropic is a real company with real economics. It doesn't have Google's infinite compute or X's willingness to burn cash on AI as an engagement play. It has to charge money, manage capacity, and make the product worth paying for. That sounds like a disadvantage. I think it's the opposite.

## The Constraint Forcing Function

When a company has limited resources, every feature has to earn its place. There's no room for "ship it and see" — what gets built has to retain paying users. This creates a tight feedback loop: if the product isn't delivering value, people cancel, and the company feels it immediately.

Look at Anthropic's shipping pace over the last year. Sub-agents, Claude Code, the desktop app, Cowork, skills, MCP — each of these extends what the product can actually *do* in ways that matter to the people paying for it. That's not a company coasting on distribution. That's a company fighting to justify every subscription.

Contrast this with Google. Gemini is competent. The Gemini CLI works. And then... it kind of just sits there. There's no urgency to iterate because Gemini doesn't need to be a standalone business. It just needs to exist inside the Google ecosystem. The AI doesn't have to stand on its own merit — it just has to keep you on the platform.

## The Graveyard Problem

There's a website called [killedbygoogle.com](https://killedbygoogle.com) that catalogs over 250 products Google has shut down. Many of them weren't bad — Google Reader had a devoted user base, Inbox was genuinely better than Gmail in several ways, Wave was ahead of its time. These products died because when you have infinite resources, the decision to keep or kill something becomes a matter of internal politics rather than user value. Some VP's priorities shift, the team gets reassigned, and a product that millions relied on just vanishes.

That's a luxury only a company with effectively unlimited resources has: the luxury of indifference.

And here's the thing — that's actually *worse* for the user than rate limits. A rate limit is friction. A killed product is betrayal. You invested your workflow into something and it's gone. Nobody wants to build on a platform that might not exist in two years.

Anthropic can't do that. If developers adopt Claude Code, Anthropic can't just kill it on a whim, because that revenue is keeping the lights on. The constraint forces commitment — not just to shipping features, but to maintaining and improving them.

## The Research Backs This Up

This isn't just vibes. A Harvard Business School study of nearly 12,000 tech firms found that companies operating under resource constraints were more likely to develop unique and unconventional approaches. Companies that received large early funding became *less* innovative afterward. The researchers' framing: constraints serve as a "productive forcing mechanism."

A meta-review of 145 empirical studies found the same pattern across individuals, teams, and organizations — a healthy dose of constraints benefits creativity and innovation. It's only when constraints become overwhelming that they become destructive.

## The Bottom Line

Google and X can subsidize their AI as loss leaders. The AI doesn't have to justify its own existence — it just has to keep you inside the ecosystem. That means there's no tight loop between "is this product good enough to pay for" and "should we keep building it."

Anthropic doesn't have that option. Every feature has to compound into a reason to stay. Every release has to matter. That survival pressure translates directly into product quality and shipping pace.

The rate limits are annoying. The outages are real. But they're symptoms of a company that's actually accounting for the true cost of what it provides — and that's exactly the kind of company I want building the tools I depend on.
