*The previous part lays out the idea from first principles: beauty is a kind of power — it acts on people before they consent — and the grown-up thing to do with power is to carry it with care, neither flaunting it nor burying it as shameful. That argument is deliberately narrow: it only speaks where power is in play, and it generates no dress codes. This part turns to a specific application: the place of modesty in Jewish life, and a failure mode the principle predicts.*

## "But Why So Many Rules for Women?"

The balance of halachic literature regarding modest dress is about women's dress. If the principle is universal — why?

The underlying law is symmetric and terse. The requirement, stripped down, is two words: *be modest.* It binds men and women identically. For most of history that's nearly all that needed saying, because the boundary was obvious and nobody inside the community was pushing on it. Detailed codification is not a measure of how heavy the duty is. It's a measure of how hard the duty is being *contested.* Law gets specific exactly where it's under pressure — where the obvious line has been argued down to the inch and someone has to redraw it. The volume of rules tracks the volume of the fight, not the location of the obligation.

Women's dress, in the modern world, got driven to an extreme. So the law had to articulate what used to go without saying. That's the whole reason the literature is lopsided — not a heavier yoke on women, but a heavier *assault* on one front, met with detail because detail was what the moment required. We don't generate secular social norms; we respond to them. So "why so many rules for women?" bounces straight back to the questioner: ask the corruption, not the code.

And men are not exempt — the principle is live for them and you can watch it operate. There's a real male modesty floor: covered torso, no short shorts, shoulders and knees as the practiced baseline. The wider world routinely goes below it — shirtless joggers, the gym tank on the street, swim trunks as daywear. The deviation is right there in the open. And the community holds the line without strain and without a thick rulebook, *because the pressure to deviate isn't coming from inside.* Same mechanism, running in plain sight: the duty exists, the world violates it, the internal norm holds quietly, and so no dense codification was ever needed. The moment male dress *did* come under internal pressure — head covering, beard, distinctive dress under the pressures of emancipation, the whole literature of not looking like everyone else — you get exactly the density the theory predicts. The pattern holds in both directions.

## The Unhelpful Cure

If modesty is the willed non-display of a power, then a community that fixates on women's hemlines — that runs full-page ads bemoaning some particular laxity, that blurs women out of its magazines entirely — has not become *more* modest. It has done the opposite. It has made women's bodies the loudest thing in the room. It has defined a woman so completely by what she covers that nothing else about her can be seen. That is the mirror image of the immodesty it claims to fight: both reduce a person to a body, one by uncovering it and one by obsessing over its covering.

This critique has been made repeatedly from inside Orthodoxy. The crux of all of their writings is relevant here, without getting into their conclusions and remedies. Avital Chizhik-Goldschmidt called it "Orthodox Compulsive Disorder": flaunting one's modesty to excess becomes its own immodesty.[^1] Rabbi Ilan Feldman called erasing women from communal media the ultimate degradation of women — objectification carried out in modesty's name.[^2] The same point has been pressed by a student writing in the *YU Observer*[^3] and in an essay hosted on Natan Slifkin's *Rationalist Judaism*.[^4]

The grown-up who declines to trade on a power is being modest. The community that can talk about nothing else is flaunting the very thing it claims to be hiding.

## Looking to Tradition

But this is where the rational argument runs out. It only speaks where power is in play; it generates no dress codes; it can diagnose the loud, anxious version of modesty but it can't, on its own, build the quiet one. That part comes from somewhere the argument doesn't reach — from tradition, from a way of life held over generations. And the instinct underneath tznius is a true and beautiful one: that a person is more than the power they carry,[^5] and that carrying it with dignity is itself a kind of strength. A community that holds that quietly — the way real power is always held — isn't weakened by its modesty. It's the stronger for it.

The rational frame would call that a case with no power in play. But the rabbis' own response cuts deeper: other women did the same, they noted, and did not merit what she did.
