## The Innocence of Children

Picture an isolated village in the hills. The children there have found little nuggets of gold in the streambed, and they play with them — flick them, trade them, leave them in the dirt. You watch and you smile. They are holding something that could build a house or buy a field, and to them it is a pebble that catches the light.

That is innocence. It is *not knowing the value of what you hold.* The child who throws a heavy object at his brother's head and misses, breaking a window instead, is guilty of the broken window and the thrown object both. And he is still innocent, because he cannot yet feel the weight of what he nearly did. He doesn't see how close he came. The depth of harm, the full meaning of being responsible for another person — these are things a child develops into. Empathy at that depth is earned, not issued at birth. So a child can be guilty and innocent at once: he did the thing, but the thing has no real size to him yet.

Adam and Eve were naked in the garden and were not ashamed. The tree was the tree of *knowledge*. What they ate was awareness. Before it, they were the children with the gold — they held their own bodies, their own power, and did not know what they held. After it, they knew. And what they became was not bad. Outside of the one act, they sin no further, ever — nothing in the text, nothing even a secular reader would flag. They didn't fall into evil. They fell into *valuation.* They looked at the same bodies they'd always had and, for the first time, saw a price tag.[^1]

And then they covered themselves. Not from shame about the body — from accuracy about the new condition. They now knew what they were carrying.

## We Are Not Children

You can argue about whether that was a fall, a fortunate fall, or a step up the ladder. It doesn't matter here. The relevant fact survives every theological position: we are aware. We know the difference between good and evil and we choose between them all day long. We know what our actions cost. Whatever you think Eden was, you are demonstrably not in it.

Modesty is just the appropriate way to carry value once you know you're carrying it. Adults know the value of what they hold. Children don't. We are not children.

## What Power Actually Is

Imagine a bank robber who walks in waving a gun — but in this world the gun only works if the teller agrees. He has to ask, and she has to consent, before it can do anything. That gun is a water gun. It is not a source of power, because power is precisely the thing that *operates without your consent.* The robber's real gun is power because the teller doesn't get a vote. It has already changed the room before anyone decides anything.

This is the test for whether something is power or merely a gift you offer. A beautiful singing voice waits for you. You can close the door, leave the hall, decline to listen — it acts only with your cooperation. Beauty in a person does not wait. By the time you've decided how to feel about it, it has already acted on you. It restructured the room before anyone consented to anything.

That involuntariness is the entire difference, and it's why beauty is *power* and not just a pleasant quality. And this isn't a subjective moral intuition — it's measurable. Psychologists call it pretty privilege, the halo effect: attractive people are more likely to be hired, paid more, promoted faster, graded higher by teachers, and given lighter sentences by judges.[^2] The advantage shows up in the most supposedly objective systems we have. Beauty moves outcomes before anyone consciously decides to let it. That's not "men, control yourselves." That's a documented force acting on people below the level of choice — which is the definition of power.

So a woman knows the value and the currency of her beauty. This isn't an accusation and it isn't flattery — it's the adult condition applied to a specific kind of capital. Beauty is leverage that acts before the target chooses. That makes it power in the strict sense.

## You Don't Flaunt Power

You don't put on display power you hold. Not because the power is shameful, but because flaunting it is a category error — it injects a differential into rooms that should be free of it.

We already know this everywhere except the one place the conversation usually fixates on. You don't tell people how much money you make. You don't wave a roll of cash around. The rich man flashing his bankroll isn't a victim of other people's greed — he's the one doing something crass, and everyone can see it. It would be strange to defend his behavior by saying "well, people *shouldn't* be so impressed by money." That's true and beside the point. The point is that he, knowing exactly what money does to a room, chose to make everyone feel it.

Concealed carry isolates the variable. Take a lawful gun owner — someone who believes, as a matter of principle, that he is fully entitled to the weapon. Even the NRA doesn't endorse open carry. Same gun, same right, same lawful possession; the only thing that changes is whether it's *displayed.* And the considered position, from the people least ashamed of the gun, is: conceal it. Not because owning it is wrong. Because everyone in the room behaves differently when they can see it, and that's a power you don't impose on a room casually. Nobody hears "concealed carry" and concludes the man is ashamed of his gun. He just understands what its visibility does to the people around him.

Modesty is concealed carry for the power a person holds in their own body. It isn't hiding the thing out of shame. It's declining to make a whole room aware of a power that acts on them whether they want it to or not.

## And You Don't Bury It Either

Not flaunting a power is not the same as treating it as shameful. When the Israelites built the Tabernacle, the women donated the copper mirrors they had used to make themselves beautiful for their husbands in Egypt. Moshe wanted to reject them — to him they were instruments of lust, the opposite of holiness. God overruled him: these are more precious to Me than anything, because by them the women raised up legions of children in the worst of the bondage. The mirrors were made into the *kiyor*, the washstand the priests used to purify themselves before serving — the one vessel given no fixed dimensions, built large enough to hold every last mirror.[^3]

Beauty wasn't buried as shameful. The very power Moshe flinched from was the donation God called most precious, and it was made into a vessel of the Temple service — because of how it had been used. The power is not the problem and not the prize. What it becomes depends entirely on the hand that carries it.

## Embracing Confidence

Of note is that a gun holsters and cash goes in a wallet — but beauty doesn't switch off. You can't put it away. Look at wealth: nobody thinks the rich man has to dress in rags and feign poverty to be modest about his money. Everyone knowing he's well-off isn't the immodesty. Flashing the roll is. The differential doesn't vanish and it isn't supposed to — what's in a person's hands is whether they amplify it and trade on it, not whether it exists at all. The line isn't between beautiful and plain. It's between possessing power and leaning on it.

Pushed power isn't strong. The parent who states what needs to happen and is heeded has authority. The parent who yells, threatens, and slams doors to get the same result has revealed he doesn't — the noise is there precisely because the authority isn't landing on its own. Same with the boss everyone knows is the boss, who says it quietly and the room moves, versus the one who pounds the table demanding to be obeyed. The table-pounder is advertising weakness. Real power doesn't need volume. It's present, it's felt, everyone in the room knows it's there, and it doesn't have to announce itself to be real.

That's the same structure as modesty. The power that's simply present — and not pushed — is operating from strength. The power shoved in people's faces is compensating for something. You don't make the differential disappear, because you can't and shouldn't. But leaning on it, amplifying it, forcing it into everyone's line of sight — that's not power confidently held. It's power that doesn't trust itself.

## Where the Argument Stops

The whole argument runs on one premise: power held should be carried with care. So it only reaches cases where power is actually present. Where nothing acts on anyone — an obviously fake gun, dress that moves nothing in the room — this framework is not useful, and so it says nothing. It doesn't permit those cases and it doesn't condemn them; it's simply silent.

But "there's no power here" is a claim that has to be earned, not assumed. The whole point of the pretty-privilege research is that the power operates below the level of anyone's awareness — it moves hiring decisions and verdicts from people who would swear it didn't touch them. So "I don't feel any power in play" proves nothing; that's exactly how this kind of power works. The genuinely powerless cases are real, but difficult to identify with objective clarity across the board.

No practical rules about dress codes and the like fall out of this. It's an idea, not a code. How to apply it is a separate question this essay doesn't answer.

## The Whole of It

*Men will notice immodest dress.* It's not false. But it's the surface of the thing, and stated alone it's insulting in both directions — it treats the woman as a hazard to be contained and the man as an animal who can't be trusted near her. It puts the entire weight on one party managing the other's failure.

The one who carries the power stewards its display. The one in the room disciplines his response. Both hold an end of it; neither is reduced to a stimulus or a beast. An ethical model that put it all on the woman's covering would be the exact mirror image of the one that puts it all on the man's weakness — both make one person the problem and the other a passive force of nature. Neither is true.

What's true is older and quieter than either. We ate from the tree. We know what we're holding. And the grown-up thing to do with power is to carry it with care and discretion.

[^1]: Adam's innocence was ignorance — he didn't know what he held. That can't be recovered; the knowledge doesn't un-happen. But a second innocence is available, and it's arguably higher: the adult who knows exactly what they hold and declines to trade on it. The child doesn't flaunt the gold because it means nothing to him; the adult who doesn't flaunt it chose not to, with full knowledge. Edenic nakedness was innocence handed out for free; covered dignity is innocence by choice.
