> Social security vs investing almost makes sense,except it's barely solvent ,
> Would you put the odds of my getting the full payout from ss at about zero?

 I wouldn't put it at zero, but you're right to be skeptical about the "full payout" part.

**Current trajectory:**
- Social Security trust fund reserves are projected to be depleted around 2033-2035
- At that point, incoming payroll taxes would only cover about 75-80% of scheduled benefits
- This isn't insolvency in the bankruptcy sense—it's a funding shortfall

**Realistic scenarios for someone your age:**

1. **Benefit cuts (most likely):** You get something like 75-85% of currently promised benefits
2. **Means testing:** Higher earners like you get reduced benefits while lower earners protected
3. **Raised retirement age:** Full benefits pushed to 69-70 instead of 67
4. **Payroll tax increases:** They fix the funding gap by raising taxes (less likely given political resistance)
5. **Some combination:** Modest cuts + modest tax increases + retirement age adjustment

**My actual assessment:**
You'll almost certainly get *something*, but betting on 100% of currently promised benefits is naive. Figure 70-80% of stated benefits is more realistic for planning purposes.

The real issue is political paralysis—fixing SS requires either tax increases or benefit cuts, both politically toxic. Congress will likely wait until crisis point (2033-ish) then implement some emergency hybrid solution.

**For your planning:** 
Treating the index fund scenario as your primary retirement plan and SS as a potential bonus/safety net (at reduced levels) is the more prudent approach. Don't count on zero, but don't count on the full amount either.

> How likely is a revolution, as the middle class gets ground to nothing?

 **Short answer:** Very unlikely in the classical "overthrow the government" sense. But we're already seeing fragmentary forms of social breakdown and political instability.

**Why traditional revolution is unlikely:**

1. **Bread and circuses still work.** Even a "ground down" American middle class has smartphones, streaming, food, housing. Historical revolutions happen when people are *starving*, not when they're financially stressed but still comfortable.

2. **No unified revolutionary consciousness.** The middle class is fracturing along cultural/political lines faster than it's unifying along economic ones. People blame immigrants, elites, the other party, corporations, etc.—not "the system" coherently.

3. **Monopoly on force.** The state's capacity for surveillance and violence is unprecedented. Armed revolution against modern military/police apparatus is fantasy.

4. **Safety valves.** Democratic systems allow for pressure release through elections, protests, local governance changes. Enough people still believe the system can be reformed.

**What you're more likely to see:**

- **Continued institutional decay:** Declining trust in government, media, expertise. People opting out mentally.
- **Political volatility:** Rapid swings between populist movements (left and right). Trump is a symptom, not an aberration.
- **Parallel economies:** More cash transactions, crypto adoption, tax avoidance, working "under the table."
- **Regional divergence:** Red/blue state policies becoming so different they're functionally separate countries.
- **Chronic low-level unrest:** Occasional riots, protests, standoffs with government—but localized, not revolutionary.

**The mechanism that's broken:**

The social contract was: work hard, pay taxes, get stability and upward mobility. That's failing for many, but the failure is slow enough that people adapt rather than revolt. 

The real risk isn't revolution—it's slow collapse into something like late-stage Soviet Union: everyone knows the system is broken, no one believes the propaganda, but no one has a viable alternative, so it just grinds on inefficiently until it doesn't.

**Collapse in the "no longer recognizable as the post-WWII American system" sense:** Moderately high, maybe 40-50%.

1. **Debt trajectory is unsustainable.** $36+ trillion and climbing. Eventually bond markets lose confidence or we inflate it away (destroying savings).

2. **Political dysfunction is accelerating.** Can't pass budgets, can't reform entitlements, can't agree on basic facts. This gets worse, not better.

3. **Social cohesion is breaking.** Trust in institutions at historic lows. Red/blue divide approaching mutual incomprehension. Immigration debates getting more heated.

4. **Entitlement crisis is unavoidable.** SS/Medicare funding gap hits around 2033-2035. That's a forcing function for major change.

5. **Global power is shifting.** Not collapse, but the unipolar moment is ending. US becomes one power among several.

**Most probable 40-year scenarios:**

**Scenario 1 - Managed decline (40%):** US remains a major power but loses primacy. Dollar slowly loses reserve status. Standard of living stagnates or declines for most. Think Britain post-WWII—still important, no longer dominant. Political system muddles through with decreasing effectiveness.

**Scenario 2 - Successful reform (25%):** Some crisis (debt, war, social unrest) forces actual structural changes. Entitlements reformed, tax system overhauled, political realignment. Painful but system adapts. Think 1930s New Deal or 1980s Reagan revolution.

**Scenario 3 - Fragmentation (20%):** Federal government becomes increasingly irrelevant. Real power shifts to states/regions. "United States" becomes more nominal than actual. Still one country on paper, but functionally balkanized. Red/blue states go separate ways on most policy.

**Scenario 4 - Authoritarian consolidation (10%):** Crisis leads to strongman governance. Left or right flavor, doesn't matter—emergency powers become permanent, democratic norms erode further, constitutional constraints ignored. Think Erdoğan's Turkey or Orbán's Hungary.

**Scenario 5 - Actual collapse (5%):** Currency crisis, debt default, breakup of union, or military defeat triggers Soviet-style disintegration. States secede or federal government loses legitimacy entirely.

**The timeline:**

- **2025-2033:** Current dysfunction continues. Entitlement crisis builds.
- **2033-2040:** Crisis point. SS/Medicare crunch forces decisions. This is the inflection point.
- **2040-2065:** Whatever path was chosen plays out. Either reformed system stabilizes or decline accelerates.

**For your personal planning:**

The empire won't fall on a specific date. It'll just gradually matter less, work worse, and deliver less to its citizens while extracting more. You're watching it happen in real-time with the SS discussion we just had.

> What's a petrostate?

 A petrostate is a country whose economy is heavily dependent on petroleum (oil and/or natural gas) exports. The government's revenue comes primarily from selling these resources rather than from a diversified economy with manufacturing, services, technology, etc.

Characteristics of petrostates:
- **Revenue dependence**: Government budget relies overwhelmingly on oil/gas sales
- **Economic vulnerability**: When oil prices drop, the entire economy suffers
- **Lack of diversification**: Other economic sectors remain underdeveloped
- **"Resource curse"**: Often associated with corruption, weak institutions, and authoritarian governance
- **Boom-bust cycles**: Prosperity during high oil prices, crisis during low prices

**Examples**: Saudi Arabia, Venezuela, Kuwait, UAE, Nigeria, and increasingly Russia (which gets ~40-50% of government revenue from oil and gas).

**Russia as petrostate**: When I wrote "Russia declines into petrostate status," I meant Russia is becoming *more* dependent on oil/gas exports rather than developing a modern diversified economy. Despite having nuclear weapons and a large territory, Russia's GDP is smaller than Italy's, and it relies heavily on selling raw materials rather than producing high-value goods or services.
